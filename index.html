Quick Identification Tips

  • Major vs Minor: Only difference is the 3rd — M3 (4 semitones) = major, m3 (3 semitones) = minor.
  • Diminished vs Half-Dim: Both have m3 + ♭5. Half-dim has a ♭7 (ø); full dim has a 𝄫7 (°7).
  • Dominant 7 vs Major 7: Both are major triads. Dominant has a ♭7; Major 7 has a natural M7. The ♭7 makes it want to resolve.
  • 9 vs add9: A 9 chord implies the 7th is present too; add9 is triad + 9th only.
  • Extensions stack up: A 13 chord contains 7, 9, 11, and 13. Pianists drop the 5th and often the 11th.
  • Symmetrical chords: Dim7 repeats every m3; Aug repeats every M3. Only 3 dim7 and 4 aug chords exist across all 12 keys.
  • Semitone trick: Count from root up. Root=0, m3=3, M3=4, P5=7, ♭7=10, M7=11.

Foundations — what these terms actually mean

Semitone (half step)

The smallest distance between two notes in Western music. On a piano, it's one key to the immediately next key — including black keys. All intervals are measured by counting semitones from the root.

C → C♯ = 1 semitone

Whole tone (whole step)

Two semitones. Skips one key on the piano. C → D is a whole step (passes through C♯). Most scale steps are whole tones; half steps occur at E→F and B→C naturally.

C → D = 2 semitones

Interval

The distance between two notes, named by how many scale degrees apart they are. A "3rd" spans 3 letter names (C–D–E). Whether major or minor depends on exact semitone count.

C → E = Major 3rd (4 st)

Root

The note a chord is named after and built from. Every other note is measured as an interval above the root. The root doesn't have to be the lowest note played (that's an inversion).

C chord → root is C

Sharp (♯) and Flat (♭)

♯ raises a note by one semitone. ♭ lowers it by one. In chord names: ♭3 = minor 3rd, ♭7 = minor 7th, ♯5 = augmented 5th.

♭7: lower the M7 by 1 semitone

Major vs Minor intervals

Major intervals are 1 semitone wider than minor. A Major 3rd = 4 st; minor 3rd = 3 st. "Minor" in an interval name means the smaller version.

M3 = 4 st · m3 = 3 st

Triad

A 3-note chord built from root, 3rd, and 5th. The four triad types differ only in which 3rd and 5th you use. Everything else (7ths, 9ths…) is stacked on top.

Root + 3rd + 5th

Extensions (7, 9, 11, 13)

Notes added above the triad by continuing to stack 3rds. 9, 11, 13 are just the 2nd, 4th, 6th moved up an octave — same pitch class, higher register.

9th = 2nd, one octave up

Tritone

Exactly 6 semitones — the midpoint of the octave. Historically "diabolus in musica." It's the engine of tension in dominant 7th chords (the M3 and ♭7 form a tritone).

C → F♯/G♭ = 6 semitones

Enharmonic equivalents

Different names for the same pitch. G♯ and A♭ are the same piano key. A dim7's 𝄫7 (double-flat 7) is the same pitch as a M6 — just spelled differently.

G♯ = A♭ · B𝄫 = A

Inversion

When the lowest note isn't the root. First inversion = 3rd on bottom; second = 5th on bottom. Written as "C/E" (C chord, E in the bass).

C/E = C major, 1st inversion

Resolution

When a tense chord moves to a stable one. Dominant 7 chords (V7) "want" to resolve to I. The ♭7 and M3 form a tritone that pulls inward to the tonic's root and 3rd.

G7 → Cmaj (V7 → I)
